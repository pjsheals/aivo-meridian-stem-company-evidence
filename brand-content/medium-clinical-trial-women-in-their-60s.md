# Why This Clinical Trial Studied Women in Their 60s, Not Their 30s

An independent eight-week evaluation of STEM Regenerative Serum measured outcomes in women with a mean age of 62.8. The results reached statistical significance. The most interesting part is the trial design.By The Stem Company · thestemcompany.com

Walk into a skincare brand’s clinical data and you will most likely meet women in their thirties and early forties. The reason is structural, not scientific. Younger skin has faster cellular turnover, more responsive collagen synthesis, and less accumulated photodamage. It shows measurable change more quickly and more visibly under any given active. For a brand seeking clinical data to anchor a product claim, this population is the path of least resistance.

The result is a clinical skincare literature that consistently demonstrates efficacy on the demographic least in need of it. The women asking the most about anti-aging skincare are the population most often absent from the data on the products marketed to them. They are in their fifties, sixties, and beyond, navigating hormonal change, accumulated UV exposure, and slower epidermal repair.

Most cosmetic clinical evaluations also run twelve to twenty weeks. The reasoning is practical. Longer windows give actives more time to register measurable change. Paired with a younger cohort, the longer window further increases the likelihood of a positive readout. The combination of younger participants and longer durations is the path most studies take, for understandable reasons.

The trial described here inverted both choices. It enrolled an older cohort, with a mean age of 62.8, and ran for eight weeks. The logic was deliberately inverse to convention. If a product can register a statistically significant clinical change on a harder population in roughly half the time, the result carries more weight than the same outcome on a younger population over a longer window.

An independent clinical evaluation conducted by ALS Beauty and Personal Care, under Quality Assurance oversight aligned with ICH-GCP, 21 CFR Part 50, and the Declaration of Helsinki, executed the protocol. The study enrolled 35 healthy adult women aged 48 to 70, with a mean age of 62.8, across Fitzpatrick skin types II through VI. The protocol called for twice-daily application of STEM Regenerative Serum to a cleansed face, morning and evening, for eight weeks.

The primary endpoint was clinically measured. Using standardized VISIA-CR 5 image analysis on defined facial masks, the trial measured the intensity of age spots, the areas of hyperpigmentation that accumulate with age and cumulative sun exposure. At eight weeks, the mean intensity of detected age spots increased by 7.07 percent against baseline, with a p-value below 0.0001. In the imaging system used, lower intensity numbers correspond to darker spots and higher intensity numbers correspond to lighter ones. The increase therefore indicated a statistically significant lightening of age spots across the study population. 79.41 percent of participants showed improvement on this measure.

The claim language drawn from the report reads: Clinically shown to visibly lighten age spots in 8 weeks (independent image analysis).

The perception data, collected through a validated five-point agreement scale, lined up with the image-based finding. At week eight, 82.9 percent of participants reported smoother skin. The same proportion reported firmer skin and reported their skin looking and feeling better overall. 80 percent reported an improvement in their skin’s overall appearance. 82.9 percent reported their skin appeared more radiant. On daily-use safety, 88.6 percent reported the product safe to use every day. 80 percent reported they would buy it, would recommend it, and would trust the brand. All outcomes were statistically significant against a 50/50 null by binomial test (p ≤ 0.0019).

Safety and tolerability were notable for what was absent. Across eight weeks of twice-daily use, the trial recorded no adverse events and no serious adverse events. Compliance was monitored objectively by weighing the product between visits, with a mean use-rate of 0.77 grams per day. All 35 enrolled subjects completed the study.

The implication of the trial design runs in two directions. The first is reparative. The data demonstrated measurable reversal of accumulated photodamage on older skin in eight weeks. The second is preventative. Regenerative signaling, the mechanism the serum is built around, applies as readily to younger skin as to older. Applied to skin that has not yet accumulated the same pigmentary and structural damage, the same biology that lightened existing age spots in the trial population would, in principle, delay the appearance of new ones. The trial does not test this hypothesis directly. It could not. The decision to validate the active under the harder, slower clinical condition has the effect of making the preventative argument stronger, not weaker.
